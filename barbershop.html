What is Barbershop?

What is Barbershop?

Barbershop singing is widely recognised as being unaccompanied, or a capella, vocal music. Rich harmonies support the melody, and tonal embellishments are widely utilised, including tone shifts, pure intonation, and the little understood seventh chord. Together with other vocal styles, is what makes barbershop so unique and popular - both to sing and for audiences to enjoy.

Traditionally, a barbershop group consisted of a quartet - lead, tenor, baritone and bass, but large choirs now come together to produce these all-encompassing harmonies.

Barbershop singing has a long, colourful history, spanning several continents and eras. Often recognised as stemming from America, groups of Afro American men would gather to sing, said to relate to their exclusion from theatre halls, and in fact, famous jazz musician, Louis Armstrong talked of men gathering on street corners in New Orleans to sing.

Possessing a colourful history, spanning several continents, the traditional arrangement was for four male voices, but today large choruses enjoy the inclusivity of barbershop, with ladies groups, such as The Belles of Three Spires, increasing in popularity, too.

Barbershop competitions allow theatrics and vocal performances to blend, producing stunning shows that are enjoyed by audiences everywhere.

The Music Team

Consisting of the Musical Director and the section leads, the music team are the glue that hold the chorus together. Directing the songs, they ensure that all sections are singing in harmony and getting the most out of the beautifully arranged compositions. The music team also help with training, giving support to the members and helping them to get the most out of their voice.

Tenor Section

Within ladies barbershop choruses, the tenor is the top vocal range. From middle C to top A, the tonal quality should be light and clear, which can, on its own sound rather thin, but surrounded by others adds the sparkle.

Lead Section

Generally singing above the bass and below the tenor, the lead range is G below middle C up to C/D above middle C. Taking on the melody, every lead must be perfectly in tune and also skilled enough to convey the emotion in the composition. Often considered simple, singing lead is one of the hardest barbershop roles.

Baritone Section

The role of the baritone is considered the hardest part to sing, and a good bari must possess an excellent ‘ear’. Either sitting between the bass and lead or above the lead, the range is F/G below middle C up to C/D an octave above middle C. Filling the gaps between the chords, the baritone role can be challenging.

Bass Section

Covering G above middle C down to low E flat, D or C, in the octave below middle C, a bass can make or break a barbershop group. Providing a solidly reliable foundation for the other vocalists, the bass takes a harmonising role, making it sound simple.
